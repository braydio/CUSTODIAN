## Core Concept

The Indexers are classification invaders. They believe reality belongs to whoever names it, labels it, files it, and enforces the category. They are post-collapse ontological occupiers: armed catalogers who seize meaning before they seize territory.

To the Indexers, a ruin is not conquered when its defenders die. It is conquered when every room, object, corpse, machine, access panel, and recovered document has been relabeled under their system. They are dangerous because they are organized, controlled, and often partially correct.

---

## Core Belief

> "Unclassified matter is ownerless. Misclassified matter is stolen. Corrected matter is ours."

They do not think of themselves as raiders. They think of themselves as restorers of order. Their tragedy is that their order is parasitic. They do not preserve truth; they overwrite it with usable structure.

---

## Relationship to the Severing / Unnarrival

The Indexers believe the Severing happened because the universe was insufficiently classified. To them, Unnarrival was not divine revelation or cosmic horror. It was administrative failure at impossible scale.

Messages failed to arrive because chains were not enforced. People failed to arrive because identities were not pinned. Histories scattered because categories were loose. Truth decayed because records were allowed to remain uncertain.

The Indexers are not trying to understand the wound. They are trying to label the wound until it behaves.

---

## What They Are Right About

The Severing did create catastrophic ambiguity. Provenance failure did matter. If no one can classify what survived, restoration becomes dangerous. The Indexers understand that unstructured fragments become lethal.

---

## What They Are Wrong About

They believe a false classification is better than an open question. They would rather impose a complete map of lies than admit the universe contains unresolved damage.

---

## Relationship to Ash-Bell

They are extremely interested in Ash-Bell evidence. They try to seize it, label it, suppress it, or force it into categories before its meaning can destabilize their system. To them, Ash-Bell is not sacred. It is a dangerous unresolved classification.

---

## Relationship to the Unnarrival

They may encounter the term earlier than the player, but they redact, encode, or misclassify it. Possible Indexer handling:
- `ARRIVAL FAILURE: CATEGORY SEALED`
- `ASH-BELL SUBTYPE: NONSTANDARD`
- `UNNARRIVAL TERM: SUPPRESSED`
- `DO NOT USE LOCAL NOMENCLATURE`

---

## Relationship to the Hidden Entity

Indexers are dangerous because they might try to classify the unclassifiable. If they attempt to name or index the hidden entity, they may create a route to it. They do not understand that some recipient fields were removed for safety.

---

## Transitive Relationship to the Custodian

The Custodian tolerates uncertainty. The Indexers do not. They oppose him because he preserves ambiguity, contradiction, and incompleteness when evidence demands it. They see him as a failed classifier with obsolete authority.

---

## Visual Identity

Semi-maintained, organized, field-functional. They wear layered coats, utility aprons, segmented archive armor, satchels, docket cases, seal tabs, coded strips, numbered plates, scanner lenses, tag dispensers, impound hooks, marking rifles, retrieval tools. They should not look filthy or feral — their gear is worn but maintained.

**Color language:** Dull steel, dirty white, faded yellow, soot gray, black, institutional tan, red classification marks, pale cyan scanner glow, orange correction paint.

## Environmental Footprint

Indexers make spaces legible in the wrong way. They leave relabeled doors, numbered walls, corrected room plates, sorted salvage piles, tagged corpses, marked crates, cabinets organized after a fire, archive shelves rearranged by false category, old signage painted over with new taxonomy. They rarely destroy terminals — they alter them.

---

## Enemy Behavior

**Pre-combat:** Tag walls, scan terminals, remove labels, replace room plates, sort crates, examine bodies, mark old machines, carry objects between rooms.

**In combat:** Prioritize terminals, archive nodes, relay interfaces, storage crates, knowledge-bearing props. May retreat with stolen context rather than fight to the death.

---

## Gameplay Identity

Indexers threaten future knowledge quality. If they succeed, they cause corrupted room labels, reduced scan confidence, false map metadata, stolen archive fragments, misleading Hub interpretations, contaminated recovery rewards. **Their damage is epistemic.**
