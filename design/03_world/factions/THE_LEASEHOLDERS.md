## Core Concept

The Leaseholders are armed legal continuity. They descend from logistics authorities, access controllers, route enforcement offices, tenancy systems, freight adjudicators, and contract militias. They believe the old world did not die because truth failed. They believe it died because rightful access chains were violated.

They are not cultists. They are not raiders. They are bureaucratic violence in uniform. Their horror is that they may sometimes be legally correct by dead standards.

---

## Core Belief

> "Nothing is abandoned if a claim survives."

They treat every door, terminal, crate, road, machine, and corpse as part of an ownership chain. If they cannot prove ownership, they seize it pending adjudication.

---

## Relationship to the Severing / Unnarrival

The Leaseholders believe the Severing was an access-chain catastrophe. To them, civilization failed because rightful claims were broken. Ships did not arrive because route authority failed. Relief did not arrive because custody broke. Shelters failed because claims became unresolved.

They interpret the Unnarrival as the greatest breach of contract in history. Their answer is enforcement.

---

## What They Are Right About

Infrastructure does require authority. Routes, depots, gates, relays, and shelters need access control. Without custody, systems become predatory or useless. The Leaseholders understand that civilization depends on obligation.

---

## What They Are Wrong About

They confuse dead contracts with living responsibility. They would enforce an ancient claim over a present need. They would lock a water purifier because the ownership chain is unresolved.

---

## Relationship to Ash-Bell

They treat Ash-Bell as a disputed authority action. To them, the question is not cosmic or moral — it is procedural:
- Was the closure valid?
- Who held route authority?
- Was the warrant lawful?
- Did Custodian authority exceed jurisdiction?

They may possess legal fragments that prove parts of the truth while missing the horror beneath it.

---

## Relationship to the Unnarrival

They do not use the term unless exposed to Ash-Bell material. Even then, they reduce it to: route denial, non-arrival claim, failed delivery, access dispute, closure warrant, breach of continuity obligation. They flatten cosmic dread into legal machinery.

---

## Relationship to the Hidden Entity

They almost certainly do not understand it. But their records may contain the shape of the cover-up: null warrants, sealed route orders, emergency jurisdiction transfers, denial authority, black-office signatures. They may guard the paperwork that reveals the hidden authority existed.

---

## Transitive Relationship to the Custodian

The Custodian acts under emergency continuity doctrine. The Leaseholders ask: by whose lease, whose title, whose access grant, whose route authority? They oppose him because his restoration authority bypasses their claim system.

---

## Visual Identity

Disciplined, severe, maintained. Long coats, structured uniforms, freight enforcement armor, badge plates, claim seals, route tabs, warrant cylinders, lock keys, docket cases, access tools, confiscation harnesses. They should look like repo agents from a dead interstellar bureaucracy.

**Color language:** Dark navy, blackened steel, gray, brown leather, oxidized brass, faded gold, red seal wax, muted white markings, restricted-zone striping.

## Environmental Footprint

Leaseholders make spaces look seized. They leave claim notices, sealed doors, red wax marks, chain locks, route markers, inventory tags, barricades with authorization placards, confiscated piles, stamped crates, controlled access lanes. Unlike Indexers, they do not relabel meaning — they assert authority.

---

## Inspect Lines

- "THE LOCK IS NEWER THAN THE DOOR."
- "THE CONTRACT OUTLIVED THE WORLD."
- "A CLAIM SEAL COVERS THE WARNING."
- "THE ROUTE IS CLOSED BY DEAD AUTHORITY."
- "THE WATER PUMP HAS BEEN IMPOUNDED."
- "THIS WARRANT HAS NO PUBLIC ISSUER."

---

## Enemy Behavior

**Pre-combat:** Inspect locks, guard sealed crates, post claim notices, move through checkpoint routes, escort confiscated goods, activate access controls, secure doors behind them.

**In combat:** Lock paths, deny rooms, guard assets, retreat in formation, use cover discipline, attempt to seize objectives.

---

## Gameplay Identity

Leaseholders attack access. They create locked doors, temporary route denial, objective delays, system lockouts, confiscated items, restricted repair access, sealed loot rooms, forced detours. **Their damage is territorial and procedural.**
