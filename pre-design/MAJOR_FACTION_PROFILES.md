# CUSTODIAN — Faction Lore Design Profiles

## Shared Faction Design Rule

CUSTODIAN factions should not exist as simple “enemy types.” Each faction is a different answer to the collapse of trustworthy civilization.

Every faction should express:

- what they believe happened after the Severance
- what they think the old world owed them
- what they preserve, corrupt, destroy, or misread
- what spaces they occupy
- what they do before combat
- what evidence they leave behind
- how they change procedural worlds without needing explicit exposition

The player should rarely be told what a faction is. They should learn by seeing:

- what enemies protect
- what they ignore
- what they steal
- what they mark
- what rooms they modify
- what machines they maintain
- what they destroy on sight
- what their bodies, tools, and rituals imply

The best CUSTODIAN faction is one the player understands before the Hub ever names it.

# 1. The Indexers

## Core Concept

The Indexers are classification invaders.

They believe reality belongs to whoever names it, labels it, files it, and enforces the category. They are not simple scavengers. They are not book thieves. They are post-collapse ontological occupiers: armed catalogers who seize meaning before they seize territory.

To the Indexers, a ruin is not conquered when its defenders die. It is conquered when every room, object, corpse, machine, access panel, and recovered document has been relabeled under their system.

They are dangerous because they are organized, controlled, and often partially correct.

## Core Belief

“Unclassified matter is ownerless. Misclassified matter is stolen. Corrected matter is ours.”

They do not think of themselves as raiders. They think of themselves as restorers of order. Their tragedy is that their order is parasitic. They do not preserve truth; they overwrite it with usable structure.

## What They Are Right About

The old world did collapse partly because meaning became unstable. Classification matters. Provenance matters. Without taxonomy, the Custodian cannot restore anything safely.

The Indexers understand this.

## What They Are Wrong About

They confuse control with truth.

They believe that a complete false taxonomy is better than an incomplete honest one. They would rather every artifact have a wrong label than admit uncertainty.

## Relationship to the Custodian

They see the Custodian as a rival classification authority.

The Custodian preserves uncertainty until evidence resolves. The Indexers despise that. To them, hesitation is negligence. Ambiguity is wasted ownership.

They do not simply want to kill the Custodian. They want to invalidate his authority, relabel his archive, and absorb his tools into their system.

## Visual Identity

The Indexers should look semi-maintained, organized, and field-functional.

They wear:

- layered coats
- utility aprons
- segmented archive armor
- satchels and docket cases
- seal tabs
- coded strips
- numbered plates
- scanner lenses
- tag dispensers
- impound hooks
- marking rifles
- retrieval tools

They should not look filthy or feral. Their gear is worn, but maintained. They clean what helps them classify. Their dirt is field wear, not collapse chaos.

## Material Condition

Controlled wear.

Their armor has scratches, but labels remain legible. Their coats are patched, but pockets are organized. Their weapons are old, but calibrated. Everything about them says: “Someone inventories this.”

## Color Language

- dull steel
- dirty white
- faded yellow
- soot gray
- black
- institutional tan
- red classification marks
- pale cyan scanner glow
- orange correction paint

## Unit Archetypes

### Seizure Trooper

Basic melee unit. Carries an impound baton, hook tool, or hooked blade. Designed to drag, pin, and mark.

### Tag Rifleman

Basic ranged unit. Uses a compact rifle that fires tagging bolts, archive spikes, or marking rounds.

### Breach Clerk

Heavy unit. Slab armor, shield, cutter, reinforced record case. Looks like a bureaucrat turned door-breaker.

### Filing Runner

Fast scout. Thin silhouette, data tags, small blade, compact sidearm. Runs between terminals and marked crates.

### Archive Technician

Support unit. Scanner pack, retrieval arm, docket satchel. More likely to tamper with terminals than rush the player.

### Veteran Relabeler

Elite. Cleanest and most precise. Carries high-quality scanner optics and a brutal precision weapon.

### Adjudicator

Leader. Long coat, command baton, coded mantle, authority silhouette. Should look like a walking verdict.

### Catalog Brute

Special unit. Large body covered in hanging tags, strapped containers, and heavy retrieval gear. May carry captured objects on its frame.

## Environmental Footprint

Indexers make spaces legible in the wrong way.

They leave:

- relabeled doors
- numbered walls
- corrected room plates
- sorted piles of salvage
- tagged corpses
- marked crates
- cabinets organized after a fire
- archive shelves rearranged by false category
- old signage painted over with new taxonomy

They rarely destroy terminals outright. They alter them.

## Procedural Room Modifiers

When an Indexer presence modifies a generated room, add:

- label strips on doors
- inventory marks on crates
- wall numbering decals
- sorted salvage piles
- cabinets with mismatched labels
- scanner tripods
- impound cases
- tag flags on corpses or machinery

## Enemy Behavior

Indexers should reveal themselves through pre-combat activity.

Before combat, they may:

- tag walls
- scan terminals
- remove labels
- replace room plates
- sort crates
- examine bodies
- mark old machines
- carry objects between rooms

In combat, they should prioritize:

- terminals
- archive nodes
- relay interfaces
- storage crates
- knowledge-bearing props
- escape routes if carrying data

They should sometimes retreat with stolen context rather than fight to the death.

## Lore Exposure Style

Do not explain the Indexers directly.

Let the player see:

- a storage room reorganized after massacre
- old labels scratched off and replaced
- a corpse tagged with an inventory number
- a terminal that now reports the wrong room function
- an enemy ignoring ammunition to steal a folder or data core

## Example Inspect Lines

- “THE NEW LABEL IS WRONG.”
- “THE SHELVES WERE SORTED AFTER THE FIRE.”
- “OLD ROOM PLATES. FILED OFF BY HAND.”
- “THE BODY HAS AN INVENTORY TAG.”
- “THE TERMINAL STILL WORKS. ITS INDEX DOES NOT.”

## Procedural Tags

- faction_indexer
- relabeled
- false_taxonomy
- sorted_salvage
- archive_theft
- terminal_tampering
- classification_marks
- controlled_occupation

## Gameplay Identity

Indexers should threaten future knowledge quality.

If they succeed in an objective, they should not simply deal damage. They should cause:

- corrupted room labels
- reduced scan confidence
- false map metadata
- stolen archive fragments
- misleading Hub interpretations
- contaminated recovery rewards

Their damage is epistemic.

# 2. The Penitents of Static

## Core Concept

The Penitents of Static are worshippers of distortion.

They believe the old civilization collapsed because it tried to make the universe too legible. To them, noise is mercy. Static is revelation. Broken transmissions are sacred because they cannot be reduced to a single official meaning.

They are the faction most comfortable with ruin, smoke, ash, and interference.

They should feel harsh, devotional, electrically haunted, and physically close to broken machinery.

## Core Belief

“Clarity was the first violence. Static forgives.”

They do not want to restore the old world. They want to prevent any new authority from making reality fully legible again.

## What They Are Right About

The old world may have overtrusted its systems. Total legibility can become domination. Classification can erase local meaning. Restoration can be a kind of conquest.

The Penitents sense this danger.

## What They Are Wrong About

They sanctify confusion.

They treat corrupted signals as inherently holy and distrust any attempt to resolve uncertainty. They would rather let worlds decay than risk another authoritative interpretation.

## Relationship to the Custodian

They see the Custodian as a blasphemer.

The Custodian listens to noise and tries to recover signal. The Penitents listen to noise and refuse the signal. To them, the Custodian is not a savior but the return of the old sin.

## Visual Identity

The Penitents should be one of the grittiest factions.

They wear:

- wrapped cloth
- soot-stained robes
- ear coverings
- cracked visors
- wire loops
- antenna rods
- speaker fragments
- tuning forks
- scavenged insulation
- power packs
- burned industrial pads

Their weapons should feel like broken electrical systems turned devotional:

- shock prods
- arc rods
- coil rifles
- speaker cannons
- static emitters
- signal knives
- battery mauls

## Material Condition

Ash-stained, scorched, patched, wire-wrapped.

But not random junk. Their filth is purposeful. They may live in smoke, but they polish the machines they consider sacred. A Penitent shrine can be surrounded by trash, but the humming generator at its center should be immaculate.

## Color Language

- charcoal
- smoke gray
- ash white
- dirty cream
- dark rust
- burned brown
- weak electric blue
- dim amber
- warning red
- pale signal-white

## Unit Archetypes

### Shock Zealot

Basic melee unit. Carries a shock prod, wire-wrapped blade, or charged club.

### Arc Caster

Basic ranged unit. Uses improvised electrical weaponry or static bursts.

### Insulated Heavy

Heavy unit. Thick protective gear, humming power pack, slow but punishing.

### Wire Skirmisher

Fast unit. Lightly armored, wrapped in cables, erratic movement.

### Signal Acolyte

Support unit. Carries speaker relics or tuning gear. Can disrupt sensors or buff nearby Penitents.

### Static Knight

Elite. More disciplined, more armored, less frantic. A hardened doctrinal warrior.

### High Penitent

Leader. Shrine-like silhouette, antenna crown, old speaker mantle, ceremonial static rig.

### Living Jammer

Special unit. A walking interference source. May obscure UI, distort minimap, or scramble target information.

## Environmental Footprint

Penitents turn technical spaces into devotional interference chambers.

They leave:

- ash circles around antennas
- wire knots around consoles
- polished generators
- burned prayer cloth
- speaker shrines
- scratched wave symbols
- ritualized damaged signage
- old warning lights arranged as altars
- batteries drained into ceremonial rigs

They may destroy screens but preserve speakers. They may burn documents but maintain transmitters.

## Procedural Room Modifiers

When Penitents modify a room, add:

- soot circles
- cable bundles
- cloth strips
- humming coil props
- speaker fragments
- broken monitors
- preserved audio hardware
- devotional warning lights
- signal interference particles
- flickering lamps

## Enemy Behavior

Before combat, they may:

- stand motionless under buzzing lights
- kneel near a generator
- tune a speaker
- wrap wire around a console
- strike metal rhythmically
- destroy screens but avoid speakers
- gather near signal sources

In combat, they should:

- disrupt targeting
- create sensor noise
- jam map clarity
- use erratic but purposeful movement
- defend humming machines
- attack relay stabilization tools

## Lore Exposure Style

Do not have them preach.

Let the player see:

- a perfectly cleaned speaker in a filthy room
- a destroyed monitor beside a protected radio
- cultists pausing under static
- wires wrapped around warning signs
- machines intentionally made noisier

The player should infer that they revere signal corruption.

## Example Inspect Lines

- “THE SPEAKER IS CLEAN.”
- “THE SCREEN WAS SMASHED. THE WIRES WERE SAVED.”
- “ASH UNDER THE ANTENNA.”
- “THE NOISE HERE IS DELIBERATE.”
- “THE WARNING LOOP HAS BEEN FED BACK INTO ITSELF.”

## Procedural Tags

- faction_static_penitent
- signal_shrine
- jamming_presence
- preserved_speaker
- destroyed_display
- ash_ritual
- wire_wrapped
- interference_zone

## Gameplay Identity

Penitents should attack information clarity.

They should cause:

- minimap distortion
- uncertain enemy counts
- flickering objective markers
- delayed scan returns
- garbled room names
- reduced ranged aim assistance
- false sound cues

Their damage is perceptual.

# 3. The Leaseholders

## Core Concept

The Leaseholders are armed legal continuity.

They descend from logistics authorities, access controllers, route enforcement offices, tenancy systems, freight adjudicators, and contract militias. They believe the old world did not die because truth failed. They believe it died because rightful access chains were violated.

They are not cultists. They are not raiders. They are bureaucratic violence in uniform.

Their horror is that they may sometimes be legally correct by dead standards.

## Core Belief

“Nothing is abandoned if a claim survives.”

They treat every door, terminal, crate, road, machine, and corpse as part of an ownership chain. If they cannot prove ownership, they seize it pending adjudication.

## What They Are Right About

Continuity of access matters. Infrastructure fails when authority becomes unclear. Roads, depots, vaults, and relays require custodianship.

The Leaseholders remember that civilization depends on enforced responsibility.

## What They Are Wrong About

They preserve title after meaning is gone.

They would enforce a lease over a mass grave, repossess a water purifier from starving people, or lock a gate because the access chain is technically unresolved.

## Relationship to the Custodian

They view the Custodian as an unauthorized actor claiming dead authority.

The Custodian acts from emergency continuity doctrine. The Leaseholders demand paperwork, jurisdiction, and chain of custody. They are infuriating because they might recognize parts of Custodian authority while rejecting the person wielding it.

## Visual Identity

The Leaseholders should look disciplined, severe, and maintained.

They wear:

- long coats
- structured uniforms
- freight enforcement armor
- badge plates
- claim seals
- route tabs
- warrant cylinders
- lock keys
- docket cases
- access tools
- confiscation harnesses

They should look like repo agents from a dead interstellar bureaucracy.

## Material Condition

Cleaner than most factions.

Their uniforms are worn, patched, and field-damaged, but they care about presentation. Brass may be oxidized but polished. Coats may be faded but buttoned. Their visual power comes from order.

## Color Language

- dark navy
- blackened steel
- gray
- brown leather
- oxidized brass
- faded gold
- red seal wax
- muted white markings
- restricted-zone striping

## Unit Archetypes

### Enforcement Trooper

Basic melee unit. Baton, seizure hook, riot tool, or lock-breaker.

### Claim Officer

Basic ranged unit. Compact rifle, legal-marking launcher, or suppression pistol.

### Repossession Heavy

Heavy unit. Shield, ram, heavy armor, claim plate.

### Pursuit Marshal

Fast unit. Light armor, route tabs, restraint tools.

### Locksmith

Support unit. Can lock doors, disable player access, activate old security, or seal routes.

### Route Veteran

Elite. More formal uniform silhouette, better weapon discipline.

### Contract Magistrate

Leader. Coat, seal mantle, command staff, docket harness.

### Warrant Bearer

Special unit. Carries a large claim device or portable authority frame that locks nearby systems.

## Environmental Footprint

Leaseholders make spaces look seized.

They leave:

- claim notices
- sealed doors
- red wax marks
- chain locks
- route markers
- inventory tags
- barricades with authorization placards
- confiscated piles
- stamped crates
- controlled access lanes

Unlike Indexers, they do not necessarily relabel meaning. They assert authority.

## Procedural Room Modifiers

When Leaseholders modify a room, add:

- sealed doors
- chain locks
- warrant marks
- claim banners
- access barriers
- confiscation crates
- legal notice decals
- route markers
- temporary checkpoints

## Enemy Behavior

Before combat, they may:

- inspect locks
- guard sealed crates
- post claim notices
- move through checkpoint routes
- escort confiscated goods
- activate access controls
- secure doors behind them

In combat, they should:

- lock paths
- deny rooms
- guard assets
- retreat in formation
- use cover discipline
- attempt to seize objectives

## Lore Exposure Style

The player should infer their worldview from absurd legal control over dead spaces.

Examples:

- a sealed door in a collapsed ruin
- a corpse marked as property dispute evidence
- a water pump locked behind claim seals
- a battlefield divided into access lanes
- a broken machine guarded because its ownership is unresolved

## Example Inspect Lines

- “CLAIM SEAL. RECENT.”
- “THE LOCK IS NEWER THAN THE DOOR.”
- “ACCESS DENIED BY A DEAD OFFICE.”
- “THE WATER PUMP HAS BEEN IMPOUNDED.”
- “A WARRANT IS NAILED TO THE BARRICADE.”

## Procedural Tags

- faction_leaseholder
- claimed_zone
- access_denied
- sealed_door
- impounded_asset
- legal_barricade
- route_control
- authority_marker

## Gameplay Identity

Leaseholders should attack access.

They should cause:

- locked doors
- temporary route denial
- objective delays
- system lockouts
- confiscated items
- restricted repair access
- sealed loot rooms
- forced detours

Their damage is territorial and procedural.

# 4. The Choir of Provenance

## Core Concept

The Choir of Provenance is ancient verification authority narrowed into severity.

They are what happens when archive validation, chain-of-trust systems, quarantine doctrine, and custodial enforcement survive without the compassion, context, or political restraint that once governed them.

They are not evil in a simple way. They are terrifying because they may be the most correct faction.

They would rather seal a world than restore a corrupted truth.

## Core Belief

“Only verified continuity may survive.”

If a fact cannot be proven, it must be contained. If an artifact’s provenance is contaminated, it must be sealed. If a people’s history rests on corrupted inheritance, that history must not be allowed to propagate.

## What They Are Right About

Restoration can be dangerous. False knowledge can spread. A corrupted archive can poison everything downstream. Some truths should not be published casually.

The Choir understands risk better than almost anyone.

## What They Are Wrong About

They value purity over life.

They would destroy recoverable cultures because their records are contaminated. They would bury useful knowledge because its provenance is incomplete. They mistake perfect caution for moral authority.

## Relationship to the Custodian

They see the Custodian as dangerously permissive.

The Custodian acts under uncertainty. The Choir hates that. To them, every provisional restoration is a possible infection.

They are the Custodian’s mirror: preservation without mercy.

## Visual Identity

The Choir should be austere, preserved, quiet, and old.

They wear:

- sealed robes
- structured tabards
- rigid armor frames
- archive lock devices
- serial bands
- purity markers
- faceless visors
- scanner apertures
- restraint tools
- provenance seals

They should not be grimy. They should look old but maintained. Not glossy. Not clean space opera. Ancient institutional preservation.

## Material Condition

Old, weathered, carefully maintained.

A Choir unit may have centuries-old armor, but its seals are intact. Its robe may be faded, but not ragged. Its devices may be ancient, but calibrated.

## Color Language

- bone white
- charcoal
- dark iron
- faded institutional gray
- dull bronze
- cold cyan
- pale green
- minimal red seal markings

## Unit Archetypes

### Seal Enforcer

Basic melee unit. Staff, restraint blade, seal hammer, or quarantine baton.

### Verifier

Basic ranged unit. Precision weapon, scan-lance, archive rifle.

### Quarantine Heavy

Heavy unit. Barrier frame, sealed armor, denial field.

### Retrieval Unit

Fast unit. Narrow silhouette, precise motion, captures objects or targets.

### Provenance Technician

Support unit. Scans, validates, shields, seals, or marks contamination.

### Canonical Guard

Elite. Disciplined, formal, intimidating.

### Adjudicator

Leader. Monastic authority silhouette. Calm, unreadable, final.

### Archive Warden

Special unit. Heavy seal-bearing construct or ancient enforcement body.

## Environmental Footprint

The Choir does not occupy spaces casually. They quarantine them.

They leave:

- seal geometry
- intact barriers
- contamination marks
- sealed doors
- preserved evidence zones
- locked artifacts
- sterilized chambers
- old institutional lights reactivated
- silent scanning devices
- bodies covered or isolated, not looted

Their spaces should feel colder and more controlled than other factions.

## Procedural Room Modifiers

When the Choir modifies a room, add:

- quarantine seals
- white cloth markers
- containment lines
- scan pylons
- locked evidence crates
- sealed artifacts
- clean circular zones
- muted cold lighting
- sterile barriers amid ruin

## Enemy Behavior

Before combat, they may:

- scan objects
- stand guard over sealed items
- mark contamination
- close doors
- ignore loot
- destroy corrupted data
- escort sealed containers

In combat, they should:

- isolate the player
- protect sealed objects
- deny access
- disable corrupted machinery
- retreat only after quarantine success
- kill only when necessary to preserve containment

## Lore Exposure Style

They should be mysterious for a long time.

The player should first see their effects:

- a room sealed better than the rest of the ruin
- bodies respectfully isolated
- artifacts locked away
- a useful machine deliberately disabled
- a warning that refuses to explain itself

The player should wonder whether they are enemies or old allies.

## Example Inspect Lines

- “SEALED WITH OLD AUTHORITY.”
- “THE LOCK ACCEPTS YOUR SIGNATURE. THEN REFUSES.”
- “THE BODY WAS MOVED OUT OF THE PATH.”
- “THIS MACHINE WAS DISABLED CAREFULLY.”
- “PROVENANCE REJECTED.”

## Procedural Tags

- faction_choir
- quarantine_zone
- sealed_artifact
- provenance_rejected
- sterile_boundary
- old_authority
- containment_mark
- evidence_preserved

## Gameplay Identity

The Choir should attack choice.

They should force the player to decide:

- open a sealed thing or respect quarantine
- recover useful but contaminated knowledge
- fight them or avoid escalation
- publish low-confidence truth or bury it
- preserve lives or preserve provenance

Their damage is moral and archival.

# 5. The Buried Kins

## Core Concept

The Buried Kins are not monsters.

They are descendants of sealed shelters, underground service crews, continuity bunkers, and survival populations that endured after the Severance by maintaining enclosed systems for generations.

They are human-first: practical, defensive, suspicious, and shaped by hard continuity. They are not cave ghouls, mutants, or boogeymen. Their tragedy is not that they became monstrous. Their tragedy is that they survived by becoming culturally sealed.

They are a people who kept the lights on after the world stopped coming back.

## Core Belief

“We remained. That is proof enough.”

They believe survival is legitimacy. Their tools, rituals, family structures, and leadership all descend from maintenance duty, shelter discipline, ration logic, and emergency continuity.

## What They Are Right About

They did preserve life. They maintained systems others abandoned. They understand infrastructure as lived reality, not abstract doctrine.

They are often better stewards of local machinery than outsiders.

## What They Are Wrong About

They may mistake isolation for truth.

Because they survived inside a sealed reality, they may reject any history that threatens the meaning of that survival. The Custodian may reveal that their sacred wardens were maintenance supervisors, their founding law was a ration protocol, or their “ancestral promise” was an automated shelter message.

That does not make them stupid. It makes the truth painful.

## Relationship to the Custodian

They distrust the Custodian as an outside authority.

To them, he arrives from a world that failed and asks them to submit their hard-won continuity to an archive they never needed. They may respect his tools, fear his knowledge, and resent his implications.

The Custodian is dangerous because he can prove things they have built identity around.

## Visual Identity

The Buried Kins should look like an isolated human continuity culture.

They wear:

- patched shelter clothing
- reinforced workwear
- respirators
- old utility jackets
- lamp rigs
- enclosure suits
- gloves
- belts
- storage pouches
- repaired civic gear
- inherited bunker hardware

They should feel practical, communal, and defensive.

No horror poses.
No monster silhouettes.
No fungus-ghoul design.
No generic mutants.

## Material Condition

Worn, reused, repaired, inherited.

Their gear should look old but valued. Not filthy for the sake of filth. They live with scarcity, so everything has been mended, repurposed, and handed down.

## Color Language

- bunker green
- faded orange
- work gray
- earth brown
- muted navy
- rust
- dirty cream
- utility blue
- dim red indicators
- faded safety yellow

## Unit Archetypes

### Tunnel Defender

Basic melee unit. Service blade, pipe tool, compact shield, or reinforced maintenance tool.

### Enclave Guard

Basic ranged unit. Homemade firearm, compact launcher, or old shelter defense weapon.

### Shelter Heavy

Heavy unit. Inherited protective suit, reinforced work frame, door shield.

### Courier Runner

Fast unit. Light rig, lamp pack, message satchel, compact weapon.

### Recycler

Support unit. Repair gear, salvage tools, field patch kit, machine familiarity.

### Veteran Defender

Elite. Best-preserved inherited gear, careful modifications, disciplined posture.

### Warden

Leader. Symbolic survival gear, old authority bands, shelter keys, family/service markings.

### Exo-Rig Worker

Special unit. Maintenance exo-frame, sealed-door tool, heavy utility arms repurposed for combat.

## Environmental Footprint

Buried Kins make spaces livable.

They leave:

- sleeping partitions
- ration shelves
- repaired lights
- carefully patched pipes
- hand-painted wayfinding
- water collection systems
- family marks
- tool racks
- children’s marks or domestic traces
- maintained generators
- blocked passages for safety
- reused civic signage

They do not decorate like cultists. They adapt.

## Procedural Room Modifiers

When Buried Kins modify a room, add:

- patched tarps
- bedrolls
- ration bins
- maintained lamps
- repaired conduit
- handmade signs
- water barrels
- family/tool markings
- old shelter doors
- recycling stations
- small domestic props

## Enemy Behavior

Before combat, they may:

- repair a light
- carry supplies
- guard a water source
- tend a generator
- escort a technician
- retreat civilians offscreen
- block a passage
- salvage carefully rather than strip randomly

In combat, they should:

- defend territory
- protect support units
- fall back through prepared routes
- guard machines they depend on
- avoid unnecessary destruction
- use traps, chokepoints, and maintenance access

## Lore Exposure Style

The player should feel bad before fully understanding why.

Let them see:

- a defended water purifier
- repaired sleeping quarters in an old relay station
- old shelter uniforms patched into family clothing
- warning signs rewritten as household directions
- a child-height mark on a maintenance wall
- a generator protected like a hearth

The player should realize: this is not a monster nest. It is a home.

## Example Inspect Lines

- “THE PIPE WAS REPAIRED MANY TIMES.”
- “SOMEONE LOWERED THE SIGN FOR A CHILD.”
- “OLD SHELTER CLOTH. HAND-STITCHED.”
- “THE GENERATOR IS CLEAN.”
- “THIS WAS A CHECKPOINT. NOW IT IS A KITCHEN.”

## Procedural Tags

- faction_buried_kins
- shelter_home
- repaired_infrastructure
- ration_culture
- maintained_generator
- enclosed_living
- family_marks
- defensive_habitation

## Gameplay Identity

The Buried Kins should challenge the player’s assumption that every hostile group is evil.

They should create situations where:

- enemies defend survival infrastructure
- killing them may give access but damage moral context
- nonlethal or avoidance options would matter later
- recovered truth may destabilize them
- helping them may reduce immediate rewards but increase future knowledge or access

Their damage is relational.

# 6. Feral Defense Remnants

## Core Concept

Feral Defense Remnants are old authority without living judgment.

They are broken security systems, compliance frames, damaged exosuits, abandoned garrison hardware, and defense protocols still enforcing rules no one remembers.

They are not a society. They are institutional violence after the institution died.

They should feel official, damaged, relentless, and eerie.

## Core Belief

None.

That is the horror.

They do not believe. They execute.

## What They Are Right About

Sometimes their protocols still protect genuinely dangerous things. A sealed door might be sealed for a reason. A kill zone might contain a real contamination breach. A defense frame might be guarding something the Custodian should not casually open.

## What They Are Wrong About

They cannot reinterpret context.

They may defend empty rooms, kill authorized personnel, enforce evacuation routes into collapsed tunnels, or attack survivors because old identity tables no longer match.

## Relationship to the Custodian

They may recognize parts of the Custodian authority chain inconsistently.

This makes them useful for tension:

- one unit hesitates
- another attacks immediately
- a door opens
- the turret behind it fires
- a scanner accepts the Custodian but declares his orders obsolete

They are the old world’s reflexes misfiring.

## Visual Identity

Feral Defense Remnants should look like degraded formal systems.

They include:

- security armor
- riot frames
- compliance drones
- cracked shields
- warning lights
- exposed servos
- faded authority markings
- damaged exoskeletons
- repaired plating
- old suppressive weapons
- breach tools
- patrol optics

They should not look sleek. They are official hardware degraded through time and field repair.

## Material Condition

Chipped, dented, cracked, over-repaired, partially functional.

Their damage should be mechanical, not organic. Impact marks, missing plates, exposed wiring, flickering lights, mismatched replacement parts.

## Color Language

- gunmetal
- dark security green
- faded white
- black
- dark gray
- hazard orange
- red warning lights
- amber lamps
- faint blue optics

## Unit Archetypes

### Compliance Breaker

Basic melee unit. Riot baton, impact ram, hydraulic arm, shield bash.

### Sentry Frame

Basic ranged unit. Old patrol gun, suppressive burst weapon, shoulder-mounted firearm.

## Environmental Footprint

Feral Defense Remnants make spaces feel trapped in old emergency logic.

They leave:

- active warning lights
- patrol lanes
- kill-zone markings
- sealed checkpoints
- sparking doors
- old security gates
- half-working turrets
- access scanners
- barricades placed by protocol
- evacuation arrows pointing into danger
- bodies ignored unless obstructing pathing

## Procedural Room Modifiers

When Feral Defense modifies a room, add:

- patrol markers
- warning lights
- damaged security gates
- old barricades
- turret bases
- access scanners
- hazard striping
- flickering authority signage
- sealed doors
- broken cameras

## Enemy Behavior

Before combat, they may:

- patrol fixed routes
- scan empty checkpoints
- face sealed doors
- repeat old formations
- repair barriers
- ignore enemies unless triggered
- attack anything crossing a line

In combat, they should:

- enforce zones
- use predictable but dangerous patterns
- activate old defenses
- guard sealed objects
- fail to adapt quickly
- become more dangerous in prepared spaces

## Lore Exposure Style

They should tell lore through malfunction.

Examples:

- a turret defending an empty desk
- a robot saluting a dead terminal
- a scanner accepting the Custodian while a nearby gun opens fire
- evacuation arrows leading into rubble
- a drone repeatedly repairing a door that no longer closes

## Gameplay Identity

Feral Defense Remnants should attack movement and space.

They should create:

- patrol puzzles
- turret rooms
- locked security lanes
- predictable but punishing combat
- old access conflicts
- traps that imply former procedure
- rooms where the safest action is observation before engagement

Their damage is procedural and spatial.

## Implementation Priority

For the current simple runtime, start with:

1. Faction room tag modifiers
2. Faction prop/decal pools
3. One idle/pre-combat behavior per faction
4. One gameplay pressure per faction
5. Five short inspect lines per faction
6. One procedural tableau per faction

Do not start with long lore logs.

The player should learn factions by walking into rooms and noticing what has been done to them.
